Section I: Normal Labor

1) Diagonal conjugate diameter in the pelvis:
a. Is a diameter for the pelvic outlet
b. Equals 11 cm
c. Could not be evaluated clinically
d. All of the above
e. None of the above

Answer: e. None of the above

Explanation: The diagonal conjugate is a diameter of the pelvic inlet, not the outlet. It typically measures around 12.5 cm, not 11 cm. It can be evaluated clinically through vaginal examination.

2) The relation of different fetal parts to each another is defined as:
a. Presentation of the fetus
b. Lie of the fetus
c. Attitude of the fetus
d. Position of the fetus

Answer: c. Attitude of the fetus

Explanation:

  • Attitude: Refers to the relationship of fetal parts to each other (e.g., flexion, extension).
  • Presentation: Refers to the fetal part entering the pelvis first (e.g., cephalic, breech).
  • Lie: Refers to the relationship of the long axis of the fetus to the long axis of the mother (e.g., longitudinal, transverse).
  • Position: Refers to the relationship of a designated fetal part (usually the occiput in cephalic presentations) to the maternal pelvis (e.g., LOA, ROP).

3) The smallest longitudinal diameter of fetal head is:
a. Biparietal diameter
b. Occipitofrontal diameter
c. Bitemporal diameter
d. Suboccipitobregmatic diameter

Answer: d. Suboccipitobregmatic diameter

Explanation: The suboccipitobregmatic diameter, measuring about 9.5 cm, is the smallest longitudinal diameter of the fetal head. It presents in a well-flexed head and is crucial for successful vaginal delivery.

4) The fetus is in a third position when the back is:
a. Posterior and to the right
b. Anterior and to the left
c. Anterior and to the right
d. Posterior and to the left

Answer: a. Posterior and to the right

Explanation: Fetal positions are described based on the location of the occiput in relation to the maternal pelvis:

  • First position: Occiput anterior and to the left (LOA)
  • Second position: Occiput anterior and to the right (ROA)
  • Third position: Occiput posterior and to the right (ROP)
  • Fourth position: Occiput posterior and to the left (LOP)

5) If diagonal conjugate is “a” cm, then obstetric conjugate will be:
a. “a”+1.5 cm
b. “a” +2 cm
c. “a” - 2 cm
d. “a” +1 cm

Answer: c. “a” - 2 cm

Explanation: The obstetric conjugate (true conjugate) is estimated by subtracting 1.5-2 cm from the diagonal conjugate measurement. This is because the obstetric conjugate is the shortest anteroposterior diameter of the pelvic inlet and cannot be directly measured clinically.

6) The longest presenting diameter in cephalic presentation is:
a. Biparietal diameter
b. Mentovertical
c. Suboccipto-bregmatic
d. Submento-bregmatic

Answer: b. Mentovertical

Explanation: The mentovertical diameter extends from the chin (mentum) to the highest point of the vertex. This diameter is presented in a brow presentation and is the longest presenting diameter in cephalic presentations.

Multiple Pregnancy:

  • Increased risk of: pre-eclampsia, gestational diabetes, placenta previa, postpartum hemorrhage, preterm labor, fetal malpresentation, congenital abnormalities, and perinatal mortality.
  • Twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome is a serious complication in monochorionic twins.
  • Management may involve close monitoring, bed rest, early delivery, or specialized interventions depending on the specific condition.

Abortion:

  • Common causes include chromosomal abnormalities, maternal health conditions, infections, and cervical incompetence.
  • Types: threatened, inevitable, incomplete, complete, missed.
  • Management varies based on type and gestational age, ranging from expectant management to surgical evacuation or medication.

Ectopic Pregnancy:

  • A pregnancy implanted outside the uterus, most commonly in the fallopian tube.
  • Symptoms: abdominal pain, vaginal bleeding, amenorrhea.
  • Diagnosis: ultrasound and beta-hCG levels.
  • Treatment: medication (methotrexate) or surgery (laparoscopy or laparotomy).

Antepartum Hemorrhage:

  • Bleeding occurring after 20 weeks gestation.
  • Common causes: placenta previa, placental abruption, vasa previa.
  • Management depends on the cause, gestational age, and severity, ranging from expectant management to emergency Cesarean section.

Postpartum Hemorrhage and Coagulation Disorder:

  • Postpartum hemorrhage is excessive bleeding after delivery.
  • Causes: uterine atony, retained placenta, genital tract trauma, coagulation disorders (DIC).
  • Management: uterotonic medications, uterine massage, surgical interventions if needed.

Instrumental Deliveries, C-section, and VBAC:

  • Instrumental deliveries (forceps, vacuum) are used to assist vaginal delivery in specific situations.
  • Cesarean section (C-section) is surgical delivery through an abdominal incision.
  • Vaginal birth after Cesarean section (VBAC) is possible for some women, but has certain risks and contraindications.

Premature Labor and PROM:

  • Premature labor is labor occurring before 37 weeks gestation.
  • Premature rupture of membranes (PROM) is rupture of the amniotic sac before the onset of labor.
  • Management: tocolytic medications to delay labor, corticosteroids to enhance fetal lung maturity, antibiotics for infection, close monitoring.

IUGR and IUFD:

  • Intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR) is when the fetus is smaller than expected for gestational age.
  • Intrauterine fetal death (IUFD) is fetal death occurring after 20 weeks gestation.
  • Management: close monitoring, induction of labor, or Cesarean section depending on the specific situation.

Post-Term Pregnancy and Induction of Labor:

  • Post-term pregnancy is a pregnancy lasting beyond 42 weeks.
  • Induction of labor is the artificial initiation of labor using medications or other methods.
  • Risks of post-term pregnancy: placental insufficiency, meconium aspiration, fetal distress.
  • Indications for induction: prolonged pregnancy, maternal or fetal complications.

Anemia and Cardiac Diseases in Pregnancy:

  • Iron deficiency anemia is the most common type of anemia in pregnancy.
  • Cardiac disease in pregnancy requires careful management and monitoring to prevent complications.

Gestational Trophoblastic Diseases (GTD):

  • GTDs are a group of rare tumors that develop from placental tissue.
  • Hydatiform mole is a common type, characterized by abnormal placental growth.
  • Choriocarcinoma is a malignant form of GTD.
  • Management: surgical evacuation, chemotherapy if needed, close monitoring of beta-hCG levels.

Q. Operations & Maneuvers?
